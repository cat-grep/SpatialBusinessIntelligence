The Question

Not all of a business's clients are, financially, created equal. Some of them are stars that bring in all of the revenue with comparatively little hassle, some bring more trouble than the time was worth, and the rest fall somewhere in the middle.

This project uses PostGIS spatial analysis to answer five business-critical questions:

  1. Who are the high-value clients and where do they live?
  2. Which ZIP codes have the most untapped growth potential?
  3. How has the firm's client geography shifted over time, and which characteristics does each year's client base possess?
  4. Which practice areas, billing structures and acquisition channels generate the highest revenue per hour?
  5. Which valuable clients are going silent and which attorneys drive the most business?

The analysis connects four database tables — contact, matter, transaction, and activity — with a ZIP Code Tabulation Area (zcta) geometry layer via spatial joins. ZIP code boundaries are changed frequently. ZIP Code Tabulation Areas were created by the U.S. Census Bureau to better study the people living in each ZIP code.

Chapter 1

Defining Value: The Client Tier System

Revenue alone is a poor measure of client value. A client who pays $50,000 but requires 800 billable hours may be significantly less profitable than one who pays $20,000 for 60 hours of work.

We combine net revenue (funds_in − funds_out) with revenue per hour (efficiency) using NTILE(4) window functions to classify every client into one of four tiers:

Star

Top-quartile revenue and efficiency. The firm's most valuable clients — high total return with low hours per dollar.

86 clients avg $27K revenue avg $555 / hr
High Value

Top-quartile revenue but time-intensive — cases that pay well, yet consume more billable hours.

182 clients avg $20K revenue avg $187 / hr
Efficient

Top-quartile hourly yield — cases that close quickly relative to their revenue, even if the total dollar amount is smaller.

182 clients avg $2K revenue avg $366 / hr
Standard

All remaining clients — below the top quartile on both revenue and hourly efficiency.

621 clients avg $1.2K revenue avg $171 / hr

All clients who are not designated as "Standard" are considered Lucrative — 450 of 1,071 clients (42%) in this dataset qualify. This national overview shows how clients are distributed across the continental US before we zoom into the firm's three core markets.

View SQL — v_client_tier 
CREATE OR REPLACE VIEW public.v_client_tier AS
WITH client_revenue AS (
  SELECT client_id,
         SUM(funds_in - funds_out) AS net_revenue
  FROM transaction GROUP BY client_id
),
client_hours AS (
  SELECT m.client_id,
         SUM(a.hours) AS total_hours
  FROM activity a JOIN matter m ON a.matter_id = m.id
  GROUP BY m.client_id
)
SELECT
  c.id   AS client_id,
  c.geom,
  COALESCE(r.net_revenue, 0)       AS net_revenue,
  COALESCE(h.total_hours, 0)       AS total_hours,
  ROUND((r.net_revenue / NULLIF(h.total_hours,0))::numeric, 2)
                                   AS revenue_per_hour,
  NTILE(4) OVER (ORDER BY r.net_revenue DESC NULLS LAST)
                                   AS revenue_quartile,
  NTILE(4) OVER (ORDER BY r.net_revenue / NULLIF(h.total_hours,0)
                  DESC NULLS LAST) AS efficiency_quartile,
  CASE
    WHEN NTILE(4) OVER (ORDER BY r.net_revenue DESC NULLS LAST) = 1
     AND NTILE(4) OVER (ORDER BY r.net_revenue / NULLIF(h.total_hours,0)
                        DESC NULLS LAST) = 1 THEN 'Star'
    WHEN NTILE(4) OVER (ORDER BY r.net_revenue DESC NULLS LAST) = 1
      THEN 'High Value'
    WHEN NTILE(4) OVER (ORDER BY r.net_revenue / NULLIF(h.total_hours,0)
                        DESC NULLS LAST) = 1 THEN 'Efficient'
    ELSE 'Standard'
  END AS client_tier
FROM contact c
LEFT JOIN client_revenue r ON c.id = r.client_id
LEFT JOIN client_hours   h ON c.id = h.client_id
WHERE c.isclient = true;

WITH counts AS (
  SELECT z.zcta5ce20, z.geom,
    COUNT(*) AS all_clients,
    COUNT(*) FILTER (WHERE ct.client_tier != 'Standard')
             AS lucrative_clients
  FROM v_client_tier ct
  JOIN zcta z ON ST_Within(ct.geom, z.geom)
  GROUP BY z.zcta5ce20, z.geom
)
SELECT
  ROW_NUMBER() OVER ()  AS gid,
  zcta5ce20,
  all_clients,
  lucrative_clients,
  ROUND(lucrative_clients::numeric
        / NULLIF(all_clients, 0) * 100, 1) AS lucrative_pct,
  NTILE(3) OVER (ORDER BY all_clients)     AS volume_rank,
  NTILE(3) OVER (ORDER BY lucrative_clients::numeric
        / NULLIF(all_clients, 0))          AS quality_rank,
  geom
FROM counts;

Chapter 3 · § 3.4

Cohort Analysis: Long-Term Value by Intake Year

Grouping clients by the year of their earliest matter reveals which intake cohorts (2021, 2022, ... 2025, etc.) generated the most revenue and the highest percentage of lucrative clients. This could allow the firm to reverse-engineer the causes of the year's productivity.

The 2021 cohort leads on both average revenue ($9,719) and lucrative client rate (56%). More recent cohorts appear weaker, but these numbers may be deceiving: cases opened in 2024–2026 may not have yet fully matured.

2025* and 2026* are partial cohorts — many matters are still active and final revenue has not yet accrued.
View SQL — Long-Term Value by Intake Year 
WITH first_matter AS (
  SELECT
    client_id,
    MIN(open_date)                                             AS first_open_date
  FROM matter
  WHERE open_date IS NOT NULL
  GROUP BY client_id
),
cohort AS (
  SELECT
    DATE_TRUNC('year', fm.first_open_date)::date               AS cohort_year,
    ct.client_id,
    ct.net_revenue,
    ct.client_tier
  FROM first_matter fm
  JOIN v_client_tier ct ON fm.client_id = ct.client_id
)
SELECT
  cohort_year,
  COUNT(DISTINCT client_id)                                    AS cohort_size,
  ROUND(SUM(net_revenue)::numeric, 2)                          AS total_revenue,
  ROUND(AVG(net_revenue)::numeric, 2)                          AS avg_revenue_per_client,
  COUNT(DISTINCT client_id)
    FILTER (WHERE client_tier != 'Standard')                   AS lucrative_count,
  ROUND(
    COUNT(DISTINCT client_id) FILTER (WHERE client_tier != 'Standard')::numeric
    / NULLIF(COUNT(DISTINCT client_id), 0) * 100, 1
  )                                                            AS lucrative_rate_pct
FROM cohort
WHERE cohort_year IS NOT NULL
GROUP BY cohort_year
ORDER BY cohort_year;

View SQL — Practice Area Summary 
SELECT
  CASE
    WHEN TRIM(practice_area) IN (
      'Civil Litigation', 'Negotiations', 'Drafting',
      'Estate Planning', 'Case Review', 'Probate'
    ) THEN TRIM(practice_area)
    ELSE 'Other'
  END AS practice_area_group,
  COUNT(DISTINCT m.client_id)                               AS client_count,
  ROUND(SUM(t.funds_in - t.funds_out)::numeric, 2)          AS total_revenue,
  ROUND(SUM(a.hours)::numeric, 2)                           AS total_hours,
  ROUND((SUM(t.funds_in - t.funds_out)
         / NULLIF(SUM(a.hours), 0))::numeric, 2)            AS revenue_per_hour
FROM matter m
JOIN transaction t ON t.matter_id = m.id
JOIN activity    a ON a.matter_id = m.id
WHERE m.practice_area IS NOT NULL
GROUP BY practice_area_group
ORDER BY total_revenue DESC;

Chapter 4 · § 4.1

Practice Area: Where Cases Come From

Across the firm's service area, practice types show no strong spatial clustering — Civil Litigation, Negotiations, Estate Planning, and Probate all appear intermixed within the same ZCTAs. Geography alone is not a reliable predictor of case type. That said, targeting specific practice areas remains feasible by layering in other client features — demographics, acquisition channel, corporate vs. individual status or language — which the following sections explore.

View SQL — Practice Area 
WITH revenue_per_area AS (
    SELECT 
        m.client_id, 
        m.practice_area,
        SUM(t.funds_in - t.funds_out) AS area_net_revenue
    FROM transaction t
    JOIN matter m ON t.matter_id = m.id
    GROUP BY m.client_id, m.practice_area
),
hours_per_area AS (
    SELECT 
        m.client_id, 
        m.practice_area,
        SUM(a.hours) AS area_total_hours
    FROM activity a
    JOIN matter m ON a.matter_id = m.id
    GROUP BY m.client_id, m.practice_area
)
SELECT 
    c.id,
    c.geom,
    r.practice_area,
    COALESCE(r.area_net_revenue, 0) AS net_revenue,
    COALESCE(h.area_total_hours, 0) AS total_hours,
    ROUND(CAST(
        COALESCE(r.area_net_revenue, 0) / NULLIF(h.area_total_hours, 0) 
    AS numeric), 2) AS rev_per_hour
FROM contact c
JOIN revenue_per_area r ON c.id = r.client_id
LEFT JOIN hours_per_area h ON r.client_id = h.client_id 
    AND r.practice_area = h.practice_area
ORDER BY c.id, net_revenue DESC;

Seasonal Demand Patterns

Aggregating new matters by calendar month reveals clear peaks and troughs in demand. February sees the highest volume (289 matters), while December dips to the yearly low (122 matters) — likely reflecting year-end slowdowns and holiday schedules.

Civil Litigation and Negotiations together account for the majority of matters every month. Notably, when Civil Litigation drops to its December low (21 matters), Negotiations holds relatively steady (30 matters) — suggesting it is less sensitive to year-end slowdowns. Estate Planning spikes in spring (Apr–May, 24–25 matters), consistent with tax-season estate reviews. Probate shows an outsized February surge (28 matters) worth monitoring for staffing purposes.

SQL — seasonal demand by practice area (aggregated) 
SELECT
  EXTRACT(MONTH FROM open_date)::int AS month,
  CASE
    WHEN TRIM(practice_area) IN (
      'Civil Litigation', 'Drafting',

ORDER BY month, matter_count DESC;

View SQL — Acquisition Channel Summary 
WITH normalised AS (
  SELECT
    CASE
      WHEN m.client_source ILIKE '%web%'      THEN 'Official Website'
      WHEN m.client_source ILIKE '%refer%'
        OR m.client_source ILIKE '%client%'
        OR m.client_source ILIKE '%former%'
        OR m.client_source ILIKE '%family%'
        OR m.client_source ILIKE '%network%'
        OR m.client_source ILIKE '%previous%' THEN 'Referral'
      ELSE m.client_source
    END                                        AS source_group,
    m.client_id,
    t.funds_in - t.funds_out                   AS net_revenue,
    a.hours
  FROM matter m
  JOIN transaction t ON t.matter_id = m.id
  JOIN activity    a ON a.matter_id = m.id
  WHERE m.client_source IS NOT NULL
),
aggregated AS (
  SELECT source_group,
    COUNT(DISTINCT client_id)  AS client_count,
    SUM(net_revenue)           AS total_revenue,
    SUM(hours)                 AS total_hours
  FROM normalised GROUP BY source_group
),
ranked AS (
  SELECT *, RANK() OVER (ORDER BY total_revenue DESC) AS revenue_rank
  FROM aggregated
)
SELECT
  CASE WHEN revenue_rank <= 5 THEN source_group ELSE 'Others' END AS client_source,
  SUM(client_count)                                                AS client_count,
  ROUND(SUM(total_revenue)::numeric, 2)                           AS total_revenue,
  ROUND((SUM(total_revenue)
         / NULLIF(SUM(total_hours), 0))::numeric, 2)              AS revenue_per_hour
FROM ranked
GROUP BY 1
ORDER BY total_revenue DESC;
Chapter 4 · § 4.4

Channel × Tier: Which Source Brings the Best Clients?

Total client volume alone is misleading — what matters is the share of those clients who are Lucrative. RERM converts 75% of its intake to Lucrative status, and Official Website and Yelp both hit 50%, despite low absolute counts.

Referral (43%) and Google (43%) are virtually tied on lucrative rate while delivering the largest client volumes — reinforcing them as the firm's twin workhorses. Spanish Google trails at 33%, suggesting room to improve intake quality from that channel.

View SQL — Channel × Tier Cross-Tab 
WITH normalised AS (
  SELECT
    CASE
      WHEN m.client_source ILIKE '%web%'      THEN 'Official Website'
      WHEN m.client_source ILIKE '%refer%'
        OR m.client_source ILIKE '%client%'
        OR m.client_source ILIKE '%former%'
        OR m.client_source ILIKE '%family%'
        OR m.client_source ILIKE '%network%'
        OR m.client_source ILIKE '%previous%' THEN 'Referral'
      ELSE m.client_source
    END                                        AS source_group,
    ct.client_id,
    ct.client_tier,
    ct.net_revenue
  FROM matter m
  JOIN v_client_tier ct ON m.client_id = ct.client_id
  WHERE m.client_source IS NOT NULL
),
top5 AS (
  SELECT source_group FROM (
    SELECT source_group,
           RANK() OVER (ORDER BY COUNT(DISTINCT client_id) DESC) AS rk
    FROM normalised GROUP BY source_group
  ) sub WHERE rk <= 5
),
aggregated AS (
  SELECT
    CASE WHEN t5.source_group IS NOT NULL
         THEN n.source_group ELSE 'Others' END         AS source_group,
    COUNT(DISTINCT n.client_id)                        AS total_clients,
    COUNT(DISTINCT n.client_id)
      FILTER (WHERE n.client_tier != 'Standard')       AS lucrative_clients
  FROM normalised n
  LEFT JOIN top5 t5 ON n.source_group = t5.source_group
  GROUP BY 1
)
SELECT
  source_group                                         AS client_source,
  total_clients,
  lucrative_clients,
  ROUND(lucrative_clients::numeric
        / NULLIF(total_clients, 0) * 100, 1)          AS lucrative_rate_pct
FROM aggregated
ORDER BY lucrative_rate_pct DESC;

WITH normalised AS (
  SELECT
    m.client_id,
    CASE
      WHEN m.client_source ILIKE '%web%'       THEN 'Official Website'
      WHEN m.client_source ILIKE '%refer%'
        OR m.client_source ILIKE '%client%'
        OR m.client_source ILIKE '%former%'
        OR m.client_source ILIKE '%family%'
		    OR m.client_source ILIKE '%network%'
        OR m.client_source ILIKE '%previous%'  THEN 'Referral'
      ELSE m.client_source
    END                                         AS source_group,
    t.funds_in - t.funds_out                    AS net_revenue,
    a.hours
  FROM matter m
  JOIN transaction t ON t.matter_id = m.id
  JOIN activity    a ON a.matter_id = m.id
  WHERE m.client_source IS NOT NULL
),
top5 AS (
  SELECT source_group
  FROM (
    SELECT source_group,
           RANK() OVER (ORDER BY SUM(net_revenue) DESC) AS rk
    FROM normalised
    GROUP BY source_group
  ) sub
  WHERE rk <= 5
),
client_agg AS (
  SELECT
    n.client_id,
    CASE WHEN t5.source_group IS NOT NULL
         THEN n.source_group ELSE 'Others' END  AS source_group,
    SUM(n.net_revenue)                           AS net_revenue,
    SUM(n.hours)                                 AS total_hours
  FROM normalised n
  LEFT JOIN top5 t5 ON n.source_group = t5.source_group
  GROUP BY n.client_id,
           CASE WHEN t5.source_group IS NOT NULL
                THEN n.source_group ELSE 'Others' END
)
SELECT
  c.id,
  c.geom,
  ca.source_group                                                  AS client_source,
  ROUND(ca.net_revenue::numeric, 2)                                AS net_revenue,
  ROUND(ca.total_hours::numeric, 2)                                AS total_hours,
  ROUND((ca.net_revenue / NULLIF(ca.total_hours, 0))::numeric, 2)  AS rev_per_hour
FROM contact c
JOIN client_agg ca ON c.id = ca.client_id
ORDER BY c.id, net_revenue DESC;

Key Findings & Recommendations

01

42% of clients are Lucrative — but they cluster

The four-tier model separates 450 of 1,071 clients into Star, High Value, or Efficient tiers. These clients are not evenly spread: they concentrate in South Los Angeles, the Pomona–Ontario corridor, and a handful of San Diego ZCTAs.

02

Target quality over volume in geographic markets

Bivariate Volume × Quality analysis reveals "hidden gem" ZCTAs with a high Lucrative share but still-low client count — ripe for further exploration. These areas, not the already-dense urban cores, could be the highest-priority outreach zones for new business. San Diego shows great potential.

03

Client geography is expanding

New matter intake has shifted steadily outward from downtown cores into suburban corridors since 2021, and the total geographic footprint grows each year. The 2021 cohort remains the strongest — 56% Lucrative and $9,719 average revenue per client.

04

$4.73M in recoverable revenue is sitting dormant

313 Lucrative clients have gone silent for 2+ years. Star clients alone averaged $30K in their last matter. The dormant population concentrates around the LA core and the Pomona–Ontario corridor. Targeted outreach could reactivate meaningful volume at low cost.

05

Referral and the Spanish-speaking segment merit prioritization

Referrals deliver $4,258/hr and are 43% lucrative — the top channel by both efficiency and volume. RERM converts 75% of intake to lucrative, the highest rate of any channel. Meanwhile, Spanish-speaking clients rival English at $4,144/hr and represent 24% of the client base, with clear spatial clustering in specific communities — a growth opportunity.

06

Fix billing mix and data gaps; diversify business development

Flat Fee matters average $4,756/hr at fewer than 8 hours each — the most efficient retainer structure. Hourly - Bonus is the weakest at $3,024/hr. Over $1.67B in revenue sits under "Unspecified" scope, obscuring strategy. Separately, Attorney #2 and #4 originated the vast majority of new clients — a concentration risk that structured team-wide referral incentives could begin to address.

Methodology

Database

PostgreSQL 18 + PostGIS 3.4. Four relational tables — contact, matter, transaction, and activity — joined to a zcta geometry layer. All geometry stored in EPSG:4326; all spatial operations run server-side. COALESCE normalizes NULL fields (retainer type, scope) to Unspecified; ILIKE pattern matching consolidates acquisition channel variants into consistent categories.

Client Tier Classification

NTILE(4) window functions applied independently on net revenue and revenue-per-hour across all 1,071 clients. Star = top quartile on both dimensions; High Value = top revenue quartile only; Efficient = top hourly-yield quartile only; Standard = all others. Results are displayed as the v_client_tier view, which underpins every subsequent analysis.

Visualization

QGIS 3.x (DB Manager) loads all PostGIS views directly and renders spatial outputs as static map exports, including the bivariate choropleth (Bivariate Renderer plugin) and temporal animation series. Interactive charts are built with Chart.js 4. The scrollytelling layout — a sticky visualization panel that updates as the user scrolls through narrative sections — is driven by the IntersectionObserver API.
